Voice Calibration

What your writing sounds like, so the journal skill can match it

Your Voice — Key Traits
Direct and conversational. You write like you're explaining to someone sitting next to you. "We make software for medium-large fashion retailers. If you started a new clothing store, you could install our software and you'd have the IT system for inventory, point-of-sale, head-office, and warehouse operations."
Dry humor and self-deprecation. "Nat Torkington has no idea what he does." "We don't have a UI design person, and some would say that it shows." "He lives in Christchurch but we love him anyway." The Testing Shift Left doc opens with a fake newspaper article quoting a developer who "declined to share their name."
Parenthetical asides. Frequent use of parens for commentary, qualifications, and side-notes: "(Or pay someone else to)", "(ie never)", "(the Ngahuia Model)". These carry editorial voice without breaking the sentence.
Honest about mess. Doesn't sugarcoat. "This process generates disappointment for customers." "People burn out." "The numbers are fiction." Marks open questions and incomplete knowledge directly: "[I dream of documenting this]", "[Get this story straight]", "[Not sure about interregion costs. Simon?]"
Concrete over abstract. Explains with real examples, not theory. "If you have 100 shirts that you paid $100 total for, their average cost is $1." Uses real names, real systems, real scenarios. The backlog doc names "the Ngahuia Model", "the Glassons Model", "the Nature Baby model" — coined labels for real behaviors.
Structured but not formal. Numbered lists and headers for organization, but the prose inside them is casual. Will use "fkall" and "fuck with average costs" when the situation warrants it. No corporate boilerplate.
Opinionated and specific. "Jeff says: 'just use A!'" "Should not be used for any other customer." "99% of discounts should be discounts." States positions clearly, attributes them when they belong to others.
